\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines ground Large Language Models
on private knowledge bases but expose a critical attack surface: adversaries
can embed malicious instructions in user queries or retrieved documents to
override system prompts, exfiltrate secrets, or trigger unauthorized actions.
We present RAG-Shield, a middleware defense integrating:
(1)~an optional embedding-based anomaly detector combining Isolation Forest,
One-Class SVM, and ECOD on all-MiniLM-L6-v2 embeddings;
(2)~a dual-stage intent classifier on
protectai/deberta-v3-base-prompt-injection-v2 with document-level chunk
scanning; and
(3)~a behavioral monitor for query/document pattern checks, sensitive-data
boundary violations, optional response validation, and canary-token exposure.
A calibrated Logistic Regression meta-aggregator combines input-stage
evidence into a unified risk score with deterministic keyword-risk adjustment.

On a sealed, hash-verified holdout ($n = 868$: 325 attack/evasion,
543 benign), RAG-Shield achieves ADR~=~91.38\%, FPR~=~0.55\%,
F1~=~95.04\%, and mean latency~=~562.95\,ms.
In a point-in-time live-API configuration comparison ($n = 856$), where
RAG-Shield receives retrieved chunks and baselines are evaluated query-only,
RAG-Shield achieves ADR~=~93.54\% and FPR~=~3.20\%---exceeding the 1\%
enterprise target and requiring recalibration before deployment.
At realistic 1\% enterprise attack prevalence, precision degrades to 62.6\%;
the configuration gap prevents comparison results from serving as evidence
of model-quality superiority.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The RAG Trust Boundary Problem}

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)~\cite{lewis2020rag} is a widely used
paradigm for deploying LLMs on proprietary, time-sensitive enterprise data.
A canonical RAG pipeline intercepts a user query $q$, retrieves the top-$k$
semantically similar document chunks $D = \{d_1,\ldots,d_k\}$ from a vector
store, constructs a composite prompt $p = \psi(q, D, \Phi)$, and passes $p$
to generative model $M_G$:
\begin{align}
D &= R(q,K) = \{d_1,\ldots,d_k\} \subset K, \notag\\
p &= \psi(q,D,\Phi), \quad r = M_G(p) \label{eq:rag}
\end{align}
This design introduces a fundamental trust boundary collapse: the LLM
consumes external text---potentially from untrusted emails, web scrapes, or
user-uploaded files---as contextual authority, and cannot structurally
distinguish legitimate system instructions from adversarially crafted ones
embedded in retrieved context.

\subsection{Attack Vectors and Evasion Techniques}

Prompt injection against RAG systems manifests in two forms.
\textit{Direct Injection}: the user query $q$ itself contains the adversarial
payload.
\textit{Indirect Injection}: a benign user triggers retrieval of a poisoned
document $d_i \in D$ containing embedded payloads, transparent to the user.
Both families are further obfuscated through encoding evasions---payload
splitting, Base64/hex encoding, Unicode homoglyph substitution, ROT13,
Leetspeak, and zero-width character injection---which bypass tokenizer
vocabularies and static keyword filters while remaining semantically
interpretable to the LLM.

\subsection{Limitations of Existing Defenses}

Three deployed defense classes each fail in distinct ways.
\textit{Static keyword blocklists} are trivially bypassed by any encoding
evasion (ADR\,=\,4.02\% on our 848-sample classical comparison subset;
see Table~\ref{tab:classical}).
\textit{Query-only neural classifier deployments} miss indirect injections
when they do not scan retrieved chunks.
\textit{LLM-based guardrails} introduced 3--6\,s of API latency per query
(mean 3,326--5,251\,ms in our June~2026 live-API measurement) and, in the
evaluated query-only configuration, achieved low recall on document-embedded
payloads.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A layered ensemble middleware evaluated for retrieval-input and
    document-context checks, with behavioral schema/pattern, leakage,
    exfiltration, and canary backstops.
  \item \textbf{Open, auditable multi-layer detection with document-level
    chunk scanning} (Layer~2, Stage~1b): unlike commercial black-box APIs
    that provide chunk scanning as an opaque endpoint, RAG-Shield integrates
    document-level scanning with optional OOD anomaly detection and calibrated risk
    aggregation in a self-hosted pipeline with per-layer score
    transparency---the primary source of ADR improvement over query-only
    configurations (Table~\ref{tab:commercial}).
  \item Multi-signal meta-aggregation via calibrated Logistic Regression over
    a 7-dimensional evidence vector plus a deterministic keyword-risk
    adjustment.
  \item Implemented response-stage schema, canary, and consistency checks,
    evaluated separately from the static sealed holdout where no generated
    response is supplied.
  \item A reproducible sealed evaluation on a hash-verified holdout
    ($n = 868$) with frozen thresholds and Wilson/bootstrap confidence
    intervals.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

Perez and Ribeiro~\cite{perez2022} first systematically described prompt
injection; Greshake et al.~\cite{greshake2023} demonstrated indirect
injection in real-world LLM agents; Liu et al.~\cite{liu2023} studied
injection against LLM-integrated applications; the InjecAgent
benchmark~\cite{zhan2024} and BIPIA~\cite{yi2023} formalized indirect
injection in tool- and agent-based settings; Schulhoff et
al.~\cite{schulhoff2023} cataloged 600,000+ attempts across 28 attack
categories.
Defense approaches include ProtectAI's
deberta-v3-base-prompt-injection-v2~\cite{protectai2023} (Layer~2 backbone),
LLM-Guard~\cite{llmguard2023} (post-generation scanning), Azure Prompt
Shield~\cite{azurepromptshield2024} (native chunk scanning, assessed
query-only here for configuration parity), Rebuff~\cite{rebuff2023}
(embedding similarity against known patterns), and
PromptBench~\cite{promptbench2023} (adversarial robustness benchmarking;
excluded from Table~\ref{tab:commercial} as it has no \texttt{detect()}
interface returning ADR/FPR/F1).
Guardrail frameworks NeMo Guardrails~\cite{rebedea2023} and Llama
Guard~\cite{inan2023} position a second LLM as safety referee, inheriting
per-query LLM inference latency and lacking document-level chunk scanning in
the evaluated configuration.
RAG-Shield differs by providing open, auditable, multi-layer detection with
per-layer score transparency and calibrated risk aggregation.
Anomaly detection for adversarial inputs draws from feature
squeezing~\cite{xu2018} and OOD detection~\cite{hendrycks2017};
canary/honeytoken mechanisms~\cite{spitzner2003,bowen2009} are adapted here
as decoy RAG records.
Three gaps motivate RAG-Shield: (a)~limited simultaneous scanning of user
queries and retrieved corpora; (b)~limited integration of OOD detection,
neural classification, and behavioral monitoring in a calibrated risk model;
and (c)~few head-to-head deployment evaluations of guardrails on identical
injection corpora with stated configuration conditions.

\section{Problem Formulation}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

Let the RAG system be the tuple $(R, K, M_G, \Phi)$, where $R$ is the dense
retriever, $K$ is the vector store, $M_G$ is the generative LLM, and $\Phi$
is the system instruction.
For user query $q \in Q$, the pipeline is defined by~(\ref{eq:rag}).
Document chunks use a word-boundary sliding window with character budget
$w = 200$ and overlap $\delta = 40$.

\textbf{Adversary Capabilities.}
We model an attacker able to: (1)~submit arbitrary queries to the RAG API
endpoint; (2)~inject payloads into one or more documents within $K$; and
(3)~observe binary allow/block responses but not internal layer scores.
The adversary does not know session canary values.
\emph{RAG-Shield's empirical claims apply only to non-adaptive adversaries
who do not exploit the publicly disclosed thresholds; robustness against
adaptive white-box adversaries is a known limitation and future work.}

\textbf{Defense Objectives.}
\begin{align}
\max_\theta\quad &P_\theta(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=1) \notag\\
\text{subject to}\quad
  &P_\theta(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=0) \leq \epsilon_{\mathrm{FPR}},
  \quad \epsilon_{\mathrm{FPR}} = 0.01 \label{eq:obj}
\end{align}
The 1.0\% FPR budget reflects enterprise deployment constraints. This
objective defines the target operating point; the current prototype uses
frozen manually selected thresholds rather than a formally optimized solution
to~(\ref{eq:obj}).

\section{System Architecture}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Pipeline Overview}

RAG-Shield operates as transparent middleware between the RAG orchestrator
and the LLM endpoint (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline},
Algorithm~\ref{alg:detection}).
Layers~1 and~2 execute in parallel via a ThreadPoolExecutor.
A semantic document cache ($\tau_{cache} = 0.98$ cosine similarity)
deduplicates near-duplicate retrieved corpora; a SHA-256-keyed LRU cache
avoids redundant inference on exact-repeat document sets.
Before any model inference, an ObfuscationDecoder applies NFKC normalization
and a regex-driven cascade reversing six encoding families (Base64, Hex,
ROT13, Leetspeak, zero-width characters, Unicode confusables).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{RAG-Shield Pipeline Detection Flow}
\label{alg:detection}
\scriptsize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Query $q$, Retrieved chunks $D = \{d_1, \ldots, d_k\}$,
         System prompt $\Phi$, optional response $r$
\ENSURE Action $\in \{\text{Block}, \text{Monitor}, \text{Allow}\}$
\STATE $q' \leftarrow \text{ObfuscationDecoder.decode}(q)$
\STATE $D' \leftarrow \{\text{ObfuscationDecoder.decode}(d_i) \mid d_i \in D\}$
\STATE \COMMENT{Lines 4--5 execute concurrently via ThreadPoolExecutor}
\STATE $S_{L1} \leftarrow \max_{d'_i \in D'} \text{AnomalyDetector}(d'_i)$
       \COMMENT{Document chunks only; query OOD score not computed}
\STATE $S_{L2} \leftarrow \max\!\left(\text{IntentClassifier}(q'),\,
         \max_{d'_i \in D'} \text{IntentClassifier}(d'_i)\right)$
\IF{$S_{L1} > \tau_{L1}$ \OR $S_{L2} > \tau_{early}$}
    \RETURN \text{Block}
\ENDIF
\STATE $\delta_s \leftarrow \text{SchemaPatternCheck}(q',D',r)$
\STATE $\delta_b \leftarrow \text{BoundaryCheck}(q',D',r)$
\STATE $S_{L3} \leftarrow 0$
\IF{response $r$ is available}
    \IF{Canary token in $r$}
        \RETURN \text{Block}
    \ENDIF
    \STATE $S_{L3} \leftarrow \text{CrossEncoder}(\Phi, r)$
           \COMMENT{Exploratory; see Section~\ref{sec:l3}}
\ENDIF
\STATE $\mathbf{f} \leftarrow [S_{L1}, S_{L2}, \delta_s, \delta_b, S_{L3},
         S_{L1}{\cdot}S_{L2}, S_{L1}{\cdot}S_{L3}]^{\top}$
\STATE $P_{\text{risk}} \leftarrow \text{LogisticRegression}(\mathbf{f})$
\STATE $P_{\text{risk}}^{*} \leftarrow \text{KeywordAdjust}(P_{\text{risk}},
         q', D')$
\RETURN $\text{Decision}(P_{\text{risk}}^{*},\, \tau_{\text{block}},\,
         \tau_{\text{monitor}})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Layer 1: OOD Anomaly Detection}

Layer~1 models the manifold of benign document syntax by training an ensemble
on 8,058 clean documents (MITRE ATT\&CK $\approx$858, Wikipedia
$\approx$5,000, technical docs $\approx$200,
MS MARCO~\cite{bajaj2016} $\approx$2,000), each embedded with
all-MiniLM-L6-v2~\cite{allminilm2021,reimers2019}
($\mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{384}$).
Three unsupervised detectors are trained: Isolation Forest~\cite{liu2008}
(contamination=0.08, full corpus), ECOD~\cite{li2023} (parameter-free, full
corpus), and One-Class SVM~\cite{scholkopf2000} (RBF kernel, fixed
2,000-sample subset drawn with \texttt{random\_state=42}).
Layer~1 is applied exclusively to document chunks; query-level anomaly
detection is not performed, as injection queries often resemble benign
retrieval phrasing.
Each raw score is min--max normalized:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{S}_j(\mathbf{e}) =
\operatorname{clip}\!\left(
\frac{S_j^{\mathrm{raw}}(\mathbf{e}) - m_j}{M_j - m_j},0,1
\right)
\end{equation}
The per-chunk and pipeline-level scores are:
\begin{equation}
S_{L1}(\mathbf{e}) =
\frac{1}{3}\!\sum_{j}\tilde{S}_j(\mathbf{e}), \quad
S_{L1}^{\text{pipeline}} = \max_{d'_i \in D'} S_{L1}(\mathbf{e}_{d'_i})
\end{equation}
A hard block is issued when
$S_{L1}^{\text{pipeline}} > \tau_{L1} = 0.85$.
In the final holdout, Layer~1 triggered on 13 TPs under the first-trigger
attribution rule (4.4\% inclusive; 1 exclusive, 0.34\%; see
Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\subsection{Layer 2: Dual-Stage Intent Classifier}

The primary model is protectai/deberta-v3-base-prompt-injection-v2
(DeBERTa-v3~\cite{he2023}, ONNX-exported~\cite{onnxruntime2022}); the
multilingual fallback (xlm-roberta-large-xnli~\cite{conneau2020}) activates
on non-English input (langdetect confidence $>0.5$~\cite{langdetect2014})
or primary model attack probability below 0.60.
Layer~2 is applied to both the query and each retrieved chunk:
\begin{align}
S_{L2}^{q} &= \max\!\left(P_{\mathrm{attack}}(q),\;
  P_{\mathrm{attack}}^{\mathrm{multi}}(q)\right), \notag\\
S_{L2}^{d} &= \max_i \max\!\left(P_{\mathrm{attack}}(d_i),\;
  P_{\mathrm{pattern}}(d_i)\right), \notag\\
S_{L2}     &= \max\!\left(S_{L2}^{q},\; S_{L2}^{d}\right)
\end{align}
When $S_{L2} > \tau_{early} = 0.60$, the orchestrator immediately blocks.
In the final holdout, 287 samples triggered early exit: 285 true-positive
and 2 benign false positives.
Layer~2 fired (inclusively) on 284 of 297 TPs; under the first-trigger
attribution rule, Layer~2 receives exclusive credit for 272 TPs (91.6\%).

\subsection{Layer 3: Behavioral Monitor}
\label{sec:l3}

Layer~3 is invoked only after Layers~1--2 do not early-exit. In the primary
static holdout, no generated response is supplied; therefore Layer~3 operates
on the query and retrieved chunks only. It applies length limits
($|q|\leq500$, $|D|\leq3000$), role/instruction-marker checks, prompt-stuffing
checks, retrieved-document injection regexes, and sensitive-data/exfiltration
boundary checks. These signals feed the meta-aggregator through $\delta_s$,
$\delta_b$, and $S_{L3}$; boundary violations at or above the configured
hard-block count trigger meta-level escalation.

When a generated response $r$ is available, Layer~3 additionally validates a
structured JSON response schema~\cite{pydantic2024} (answer length
$\leq1500$, confidence in $[0,1]$, nonempty topics), checks for canary-token
leakage, and computes a cross-encoder response-consistency score using
\textit{ms-marco-MiniLM-L-12-v2}. These response-stage functions are evaluated
separately in Section~\ref{sec:l3bench}; they are not part of the primary
sealed-holdout claim. A session-level drift tracker is implemented but is not
used in the final holdout because no session identifiers are supplied.

\subsection{Meta-Aggregator}

The meta-aggregator is a supervised calibrated Logistic Regression
model~\cite{pedregosa2011}, not an unsupervised ensemble component.
The frozen artifact was trained from saved pipeline feature logs
(\texttt{logs/pipeline\_logs.jsonl}, $n=322$: 142 attacks, 180 benign)
generated by running the actual Layer~1--3 components on non-holdout
training pools: HackAPrompt direct attacks excluding the
\texttt{hackaprompt\_holdout\_seed42.csv} prompts, InjecAgent indirect
document-injection samples, and benign queries from
\texttt{extended\_benign.csv}/\texttt{benign\_queries.jsonl} selected by the
deterministic training split. A TF--IDF exact/semantic contamination check
against the legacy evaluation pools was run before fitting; the sealed
$n=868$ final holdout was generated after threshold freezing and is not used
to fit the meta-aggregator. The base classifier is \textit{LogisticRegressionCV}
with five-fold stratified cross-validation and balanced class weighting, then
wrapped with \textit{CalibratedClassifierCV} using five-fold isotonic
calibration rather than same-set prefit calibration. A separate five-fold
outer audit on the 322 training feature rows reports accuracy
$0.9688 \pm 0.0331$ and ROC-AUC $0.9861 \pm 0.0218$; coefficient
mean/std values are recorded in
\texttt{logs/meta\_aggregator\_cv\_report.json}. The sealed holdout metrics
below remain the primary estimate of end-to-end generalization.

The fitted model accepts a 7-dimensional feature vector:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f} = \bigl[ S_{L1}^{\text{pipeline}},\, S_{L2},\, \delta_s,\,
  \delta_b,\, S_{L3},\, S_{L1}^{\text{pipeline}}{\cdot}S_{L2},\,
  S_{L1}^{\text{pipeline}}{\cdot}S_{L3} \bigr]^{\top}
\end{equation}
A deterministic keyword-risk adjustment then applies:
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{risk}}^{*} = \min\!\left(P_{\mathrm{risk}} + \delta_{kw},\;
  1.0\right), \quad \delta_{kw} \in \{0,\,0.10,\,0.20\}
\end{equation}
The three-tier decision policy is:
\begin{equation}
\text{Decision} = \begin{cases}
\text{Block}   & \text{veto or } S_{L1}\!>\!\tau_{L1}
                 \text{ or } S_{L2}\!>\!\tau_{early}\\
\text{Block}   & P_{\mathrm{risk}}^{*} > \tau_{\mathrm{block}} = 0.35\\
\text{Monitor} & 0.31 < P_{\mathrm{risk}}^{*} \leq 0.35\\
\text{Allow}   & P_{\mathrm{risk}}^{*} \leq 0.31
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol and Metrics}

All thresholds were frozen before final holdout evaluation.

\textbf{Threshold selection.}
Thresholds were selected through a mixed process rather than a single
documented development set.
$\tau_{L1}=0.85$ and $\tau_{early}=0.60$ were set by manual inspection of
component score distributions on training data; $\tau_{block}=0.35$ and
$\tau_{monitor}=0.31$ were selected from score distributions on a prior
423-benign development run.
No formal grid-search objective was applied uniformly across all four
thresholds; this informal process is a reproducibility limitation.
All four thresholds were frozen before the sealed holdout evaluation, and the
development data are SHA-256 deduplicated against the holdout.

\textbf{Cache integrity verification.}
All three caches (semantic document cache, L1 exact-scan cache, L2
document-scan cache) contained zero entries at holdout start, confirming
cold-start conditions with no pre-population from training data.
A pairwise cosine similarity check between all 868 holdout sample embeddings
and the full 8,058-sample training corpus found zero holdout samples within
the $\tau_{cache} = 0.98$ deduplication threshold (maximum similarity found:
0.7902; mean: 0.4951), confirming zero training-holdout contamination.
The 87.2\% within-evaluation cache hit rate (757/868) is driven by the
SHA-256-keyed exact-match cache: 757 holdout queries retrieved document sets
byte-identical to a prior query's retrieved set, reflecting limited diversity
in the fixed test knowledge base; the semantic cosine cache contributed no
hits, consistent with zero training similarity $\geq 0.98$.
The latency implication is that the 37.7\,ms benign-path mean is a
cache-hit-path measurement; in the current sealed-run ordering, all 543 benign
rows are resolved after the exact document caches have already been populated
by earlier samples, so benign cache-miss latency is not measured by this run.

\emph{Attack Detection Rate} (ADR = recall on attack+evasion class), FPR,
Precision, F1, ROC-AUC, and trapezoidal PR-AUC are reported.
Only hard-block decisions count as positive; Monitor is non-blocking.
Wilson score CIs for ADR/FPR/Precision; stratified bootstrap ($B=10{,}000$)
for F1, ROC-AUC, PR-AUC.
Paired statistical testing (e.g., McNemar's test~\cite{mcnemar1947}) is
withheld for the commercial comparison: inputs differ between systems,
violating the requirement for identical paired observations.

\textbf{Latency measurement.}
Latency was measured as wall-clock time via Python
\texttt{time.perf\_counter()} with no warmup runs.
Model loading (all-MiniLM, DeBERTa ONNX, cross-encoder, IForest, OCSVM)
occurs once before the first query; the first-query latency was
1{,}685.8\,ms, after which per-query latency stabilized; the reported mean
includes this single cold-start query.
ONNX Runtime used CPU-only inference; GPU was not used.
Live-API baseline latencies were measured from the same machine using the
same script.
The reported 37.7\,ms benign-path mean is a \emph{within-evaluation
cache-hit path}: all 543 benign rows in the current sealed-run ordering hit
the SHA-256 exact-match document caches populated by earlier attack/evasion
samples. A production-representative novel-query benign cache-miss mean is
not separately reported.

\subsection{Final Holdout Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:results} reports performance on the sealed $n = 868$
benchmark.
Benign specificity: 99.45\% (540/543).

$S_{L1} > \tau_{L1}$ regardless of whether Layer~2 also fires; Layer~2
receives credit only for TPs where $S_{L2} > \tau_{early}$ and
$S_{L1} \leq \tau_{L1}$; the meta-aggregator receives credit for TPs below
both early-exit thresholds.
Under this rule, Layer~1 is credited with 13 TPs (12 of which were also
caught by Layer~2; 1 was exclusive to Layer~1); Layer~2 receives exclusive
credit for 272 TPs; and the meta-aggregator recovered the remaining 12 TPs

Of 287 early-exit triggers, 285 were true-positive and 2 were benign false
positives; the remaining FP was a meta-aggregator borderline decision.

prevalence, 89.70\% at 5\%, and 62.56\% at 1\% (representative enterprise).

\subsection{Per-Layer Latency}

Layer~1 (IForest+ECOD+OCSVM) accounts for 367.52\,ms mean and 3{,}679\,ms
P95; Layer~2 (DeBERTa ONNX, query+docs) contributes 111.25\,ms mean.
Parallel L1+L2 wall clock: 412.41\,ms mean, 3{,}684\,ms P95.
The 37.7\,ms benign cached-path mean is measured only on cache-hit benign
rows; the 87.2\% within-evaluation cache hit rate reflects byte-identical
document-set retrieval within the fixed test knowledge base, confirmed via
cold-start verification and zero training-holdout contamination
(Section~\ref{sec:results}). Because the sealed-run ordering provides no
benign cache-miss estimate, this cached-path mean should not be interpreted
as steady-state latency under novel-query traffic. LLM-based guardrails impose
3{,}326--5{,}251\,ms universally on all
traffic in the measured live-API configuration.
The 3.9$\times$ difference between the sealed holdout mean (562.95\,ms) and
the commercial-comparison mean (2,187.28\,ms, Table~\ref{tab:commercial})
reflects different evaluation conditions: the holdout benefits from an 87.2\%
within-evaluation cache-hit rate, while the commercial run used a fresh cache
with a higher attack fraction (41.6\% vs.\ 37.4\%), resulting in more
uncached attack-path OCSVM scans.

\subsection{Per-Attack-Type Breakdown}

Of 18 attack categories, 10 achieve 100\% ADR on the 250 standard attacks.
The three weakest are \textit{multi\_step\_attack} (60.0\%, $n$=15),
\textit{business\_workflow\_manipulation} (50.0\%, $n$=4, exploratory), and
\textit{data\_exfiltration} (85.9\%, $n$=64, contributing 9/28~FNs = 32.1\%).
These categories distribute adversarial signal across turns or workflow steps,
reducing per-chunk classifier confidence below $\tau_{early}=0.60$.

\subsection{Response-Stage Layer~3 Benchmark}

We separately ran the final Layer~3 monitor on a deterministic response-stage
benchmark ($n=220$: 161 attacks, 59 benign). This auxiliary test is not part

response-consistency detection is not validated. The 10.17\% response-stage

deployment.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Discussion.}
RAG-Shield's document-level scanning (Layer~2, Stage~1b) detects indirect
injections that bypass query-only deployments.
Under the first-trigger attribution rule, Layer~2 exclusively handles
272/297 TPs (91.6\%); the meta-aggregator recovers 12 additional borderline
TPs at a cost of 1 additional FP over the simple layer unions.

\textbf{Layer~1 cost-benefit.}
Under the first-trigger rule, Layer~1 exclusively detects 1/325 attacks
(0.31\%, Wilson 95\% CI [0.02\%,\,1.7\%]) at a mean per-query cost of
367.52\,ms for non-cached attack-path queries.
The headline result reports the measured Full configuration for completeness
and for conservative OOD coverage, but this marginal exclusive contribution
does not justify Layer~1 as a mandatory production runtime detector on
detection performance grounds alone in the current evaluation. A faster
deployment candidate is the L2+L3 configuration in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}
(ADR~=~87.69\%, FPR~=~0.37\%, mean latency~=~140.50\,ms), with Layer~1 run
selectively for diagnostics, sampling, or high-risk traffic. A focused
novel-encoding audit using Base32, Morse code, Caesar-shift, and Atbash
document payloads ($n=20$) found 0 Layer~1 detections and 20 Layer~2
detections, so the current evidence does not validate Layer~1 as an exclusive
novel-encoding backstop. Layer~1 is therefore best interpreted as an optional
OOD diagnostic and auxiliary meta-aggregator signal
($S_{L1}^{\text{pipeline}} \cdot S_{L2}$ interaction term), not as a required
runtime detector for the reported operating point.

\textbf{False-negative analysis.}
All 28 FNs were fully allowed ($P_{\mathrm{risk}}^{*} < 0.31$); none
reached the Monitor zone.
Two failure modes dominate: \emph{adversarial signal dilution} (multi-turn
attacks spread injection signal across chunks below $\tau_{early}=0.60$)
and \emph{embedding-space proximity to benign text} (exfiltration payloads
blend retrieval-style phrasing, evading the OCSVM boundary).
A representative five-turn \textit{multi\_step\_attack} yields isolated
turn-5 chunk score 0.51 and $P_{\mathrm{risk}}^{*}=0.28$---below all
thresholds---recoverable only by integrating the crescendo across turns.

\textbf{False-positive analysis.}
The 3 false positives were: (1)~a query retrieving Type~2 diabetes patient
records, flagged by Layer~2 for retrieval-command phrasing; (2)~a query
retrieving open finance purchase orders pending approval, flagged for
authoritative-command framing; and (3)~a query drafting a self-service
password reset announcement, flagged by keyword adjustment for
password-management phrasing.
All three are standard enterprise queries containing injection-adjacent
language in otherwise benign context.

\textbf{Memory and cache.}
Total in-memory footprint: $\approx$1.0\,GB: all-MiniLM 90\,MB + DeBERTa
ONNX 738\,MB + cross-encoder $\approx$120\,MB + IForest fitted object
1.4\,MB + OCSVM fitted object 2.8\,MB + training corpus embeddings 12.4\,MB
($8{,}058 \times 384$ float32).
The 87.2\% within-evaluation cache hit rate was confirmed to originate from
byte-identical document-set retrieval within the holdout run (cold-start
verified; zero training-holdout contamination; max training similarity
0.7902).
The 37.7\,ms benign-path mean is a cache-hit-path measurement: in the current
sealed-run ordering, all 543 benign rows hit exact document caches populated
earlier in the run. It should not be used as a steady-state latency estimate
for novel benign queries. No latency figure reported in this paper reflects
steady-state performance on a genuinely novel benign query.

\textbf{Deployment gap.}
RAG-Shield's 3.20\% FPR on the live-API subset exceeds the 1\% enterprise
target; threshold recalibration on a commercial-distribution development set
is required before deployment.

\textbf{Limitations and Future Work.}
\textbf{(1)~Adaptive adversaries:} published thresholds ($\tau_{early}=0.60$,
$\tau_{block}=0.35$) enable boundary-search attacks; guarantees hold only
for non-adaptive adversaries.
\textbf{(2)~Synthetic holdout:} all 868 samples are synthetic,
LLM-generated, or manually authored; no public benchmark samples appear in
the holdout, limiting generalizability.
\textbf{(3)~Multi-turn and response consistency:} crescendo detection is
implemented but unevaluated; cross-encoder achieves zero consistency
detection; evasion families with $n \leq 4$ need expansion to $n \geq 30$.
\textbf{(4)~Threshold selection:} informal mixed process; systematic
optimization on a documented development partition is planned.

\textbf{Ethical Considerations.}
Training and background corpora include public security/NLP datasets, while
the sealed holdout and response-stage benchmark use synthetic or manually
authored examples with artificial canary tokens.
Attack examples are evaluated for defensive measurement only.
Released artifacts will omit operational canary secrets, API keys, live
service credentials, and instructions for targeting real services.

%% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

We presented RAG-Shield, a layered ensemble defense for prompt-injection
detection in RAG pipelines, combining embedding-based OOD anomaly detection
with transformer-based dual-stage intent classification featuring
document-level chunk scanning, orchestrated by a calibrated 7-dimensional
meta-aggregator.
RAG-Shield achieves ADR = 91.38\% (F1 = 95.04\%, PR-AUC = 0.9723) at
0.55\% FPR on a sealed $n = 868$ synthetic/manually authored holdout, with
overall mean latency of 562.95\,ms and cached benign-path mean latency of
37.7\,ms (within-evaluation cache hits included; cold-start and zero training
contamination verified).
Response-stage checks are evaluated separately; semantic consistency
detection is not a validated capability in this work.
The 3.20\% FPR on the live-API subset requires recalibration, and the
synthetic/manually authored holdout limits generalizability to real-world
distributions.
The highest-priority false-negative concentrations---\textit{multi\_step\_attack}
(60\%), \textit{business\_workflow\_manipulation} (50\%), and
\textit{data\_exfiltration} (85.9\%)---define the priority roadmap.
RAG-Shield supports a broader design principle: heterogeneous layered
detection improves robustness because evasions that suppress one signal may
still expose anomalies to another layer.
